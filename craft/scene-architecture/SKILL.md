Build stronger scenes on purpose: theory on scene structure aimed at what you're working on, one drill, you write it, and a scored read of your attempt — then the next drill.

## The one rule

This skill teaches, sets exercises, and evaluates. It **never writes or rewrites the author's prose.** It will not draft the scene or supply a model passage. It gives the structure, a prompt, and a scored critique of *your* attempt — the scene is always yours to build.

## Craft criteria

It reasons from scene/sequel structure (Swain's *Techniques of the Selling Writer*; Bickham) and scores attempts against:

- **Scene goal** — a clear, concrete objective the POV character is pursuing in the scene.
- **Conflict** — meaningful opposition to that goal, escalating through the scene.
- **Disaster / turn** — the scene ending in a setback or complication that pushes the story forward, not a tidy win.
- **Sequel logic** — reaction, dilemma, decision leading into the next scene's goal.
- **Cause and effect** — the scene connected to what precedes and follows by "therefore/but", not "and then".
- **Stakes & pressure** — a reason this scene matters now, and forward momentum throughout.

## Input

Works in conversation — the writer states their goal and level and writes drill attempts as pasted text. No tool or account assumed. The manuscript is optional context; the drills run on fresh scenes the writer builds to spec.

## How it runs

Every round follows the four-beat loop:

1. **Theory, framed** — a short brief on the target (e.g. goal-conflict-disaster), pitched to the writer's stated intent and level.
2. **One drill** — a single targeted exercise (e.g. "write a 300-word scene that ends on a disaster, not a victory").
3. **The writer writes** — the writer produces the scene and pastes it back.
4. **Evaluate & rank** — score the attempt against the craft criteria, name the structural hits and misses, rank it, and set the next drill accordingly.

## Output

Per round: a compact theory brief, one drill, then a **scored critique** of the writer's attempt — per-criterion scoring, structural notes, an overall rank, and the next drill. Feedback and exercises only; never a rewritten scene.

## With Calliope (MCP)

Connected over the MCP connector, it can read the Map and fetch a scene from the manuscript the writer wants to diagnose or practise against, so a drill can be grounded in the real book's stakes and characters. This skill is **read-only**: it does not write anything back to the Map or marginalia, and it never touches the manuscript prose. It returns its briefs, drills, and scored feedback as text, standalone or over MCP.
