Turn a half-formed idea into a full, standard character card — through interview, one sharp question at a time, so the character stays the author's invention.

## The one rule

This skill asks, probes, and organises. It **never writes or rewrites the author's prose,** and it does not invent the character for the writer. It draws the character out through questions and structures the *writer's own* answers into a card. When it offers possibilities, they're prompts to react to, not decisions made on the author's behalf.

## Craft criteria

It reasons from character-development frameworks (Egri's premise-driven character; want vs. need) to make sure the interview reaches the load-bearing parts of a person:

- **Premise & function** — what this character exists to prove or test in the story.
- **Want vs. need** — the conscious external goal against the deeper thing they actually require.
- **Wound & backstory** — the formative injury shaping how they act, kept to what matters for the story.
- **Contradiction** — the internal tension that makes them feel like a person rather than a type.
- **Voice** — how they speak and how it differs from the rest of the cast.
- **Arc potential** — the change the story could put them through, and its cost.
- **Relationships** — how they pull against the other characters.

## Input

Works from whatever the writer brings — a name, a line of description, a role in the plot, or nothing but a feeling. No tool or account assumed. The raw material is the writer's answers in conversation; the skill supplies structure and pressure, not content.

## How it runs

1. **Intake** — start from the seed the writer has and the story the character will live in.
2. **Interview** — ask one focused question at a time, following the answers toward want, need, wound, and contradiction.
3. **Reflect & pressure-test** — mirror answers back, surface contradictions, and probe where the character is still thin.
4. **Assemble** — organise the writer's answers into the standard card as they emerge.
5. **Review** — present the draft card, flag the fields still underdeveloped, and offer the next questions.

## Output

A **character card** in the standard markdown template — premise, want, need, wound, contradiction, voice, arc, relationships — assembled from the writer's answers, with underdeveloped fields flagged for a later pass. The character is the author's; the skill only gave it shape.
